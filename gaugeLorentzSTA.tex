\makeproblem{Lorentz force gauge transformation.}{problem:gaugeLorentzSTA:20}{
Show that the gauge transformation \( A \rightarrow A + \grad \psi \) applied to the Lorentz force Lagrangian
\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:gaugeLorentzSTA:20}
\Lq = \inv{2} m v^2 + q A \cdot v/c,
\end{dmath}
does not change the equations of motion.
} % problem
\makeanswer{problem:gaugeLorentzSTA:20}{
The gauge transformed Lagrangian is
\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:gaugeLorentzSTA:40}
\Lq = \inv{2} m v^2 + q A \cdot v/c + \frac{q v}{c} \cdot \grad \phi.
\end{dmath}
%We know that the Lorentz force equations are obtained from the first two terms, so need only consider the effects of the new \( \phi \) dependent term on the action.  
Observe that
\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:gaugeLorentzSTA:60}
v \cdot \grad \phi
=
\frac{dx^\mu}{d\tau} \PD{x^\mu}{\phi}
=
\frac{d \phi}{d\tau},
\end{dmath}
which means that the action is transformed to
\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:gaugeLorentzSTA:80}
S
\rightarrow S + \frac{q}{c} \int d\tau \frac{d\phi}{d\tau}
= S + \frac{q}{c} \evalbar{\phi}{\Delta \tau}.
\end{dmath}
As the action is evaluated over the fixed interval \( \Delta \tau \), the gauge transformation only changes the action by a constant, so the equations of motion are unchanged.
} % answer
